Customer Declined Repair Follow-Up Checklist for Local Service Businesses

CUSTOMER-DECLINED-REPAIR-FOLLOW-UP-READY — Use this worksheet before sending AI-written or templated follow-up to a customer who declined a recommended repair, diagnostic, replacement, maintenance plan, or safety-related service option.

When a customer says “not now,” local-service teams still need a safe record: what was recommended, what was declined, whether the declined work creates risk, and when it is appropriate to check back. This page gives you copy/paste fields and messages that avoid invented scare tactics, fake urgency, unsupported warranty claims, or unauthorized discounts.

1) Declined-repair source card

  • Customer / job: {customer_name}, {job_address}, {job_id}, {technician_or_estimator}
  • Recommended work: {repair_or_replacement_recommended}
  • Why it was recommended: {observed_issue}, {photo_or_diagnostic_source}, {safety_or_performance_note}
  • Customer decision: declined / deferred / wants second opinion / budget hold / needs owner approval
  • Quote status: {quote_number}, {price_range_if_approved}, {expiration_date}, {financing_or_deposit_if_any}
  • Risk class: routine / performance / property-damage risk / safety-sensitive / warranty-sensitive / manager review required
  • Next check date: {follow_up_date}, {owner_of_follow_up}, {allowed_channel}

2) Customer-safe follow-up text snippets

Plain follow-up: “Hi {first_name}, checking in on the {recommended_work} we discussed for {job_or_address}. If you want to revisit it, reply here and we can confirm the current scope, timing, and any updated pricing before scheduling.”

Risk-aware but not alarmist: “Our note says this was recommended because {observed_issue}. We do not want to overstate anything by text, so if you are seeing new symptoms, please send a photo or call us and we will have a human review the next step.”

Quote expiration: “The prior quote may need review before we can honor pricing or timing. If you want to move forward, we will confirm the current part, labor, warranty, and schedule details first.”

Second-opinion friendly: “No pressure — if you got another opinion and want us to compare scope, send the details and we can explain what is included, what is not, and what needs owner approval.”

STOP AUTOMATION guardrails

  • Stop if the declined work is safety-sensitive, code-related, warranty-denial-related, insurance-related, or could create property-damage risk.
  • Do not invent diagnosis, urgency, failure timing, warranty coverage, legal liability, discounts, financing, part availability, or appointment windows.
  • Do not use fear-based language. Keep the message factual, source-backed, and easy for a human to review.
  • Do not text if the customer opted out, the channel is not allowed, or consent/source ownership is unclear.
  • Escalate to an owner/manager before marking the lead lost when the recommendation involved health, safety, access, code, warranty, or repeated prior problems.
