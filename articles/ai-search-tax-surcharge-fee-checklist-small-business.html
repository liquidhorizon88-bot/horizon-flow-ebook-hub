Why this matters

Fee wording is a high-risk place for AI to overstate buyer cost

Small businesses often mention taxes, surcharges, card fees, delivery charges, and rush fees across pricing pages, quote emails, invoices, checkout screens, and policy pages. If those sources disagree, AI search tools can summarize the wrong total cost or imply a fee applies to every buyer.

This checklist gives owners a simple source-of-truth record before publishing fee language or asking AI to draft pricing-page copy.

5. AI-search audit prompt
Paste this into your AI/search QA tool:
"Review this fee, checkout, pricing, and policy page. List every tax, surcharge, convenience fee, delivery fee, rush fee, environmental fee, payment fee, and exception. Flag any fee that lacks a source URL, checkout proof, owner approval, effective date, or exception rule. Do not invent tax advice, legal requirements, waived fees, payment-method rules, or final buyer totals."

STOP PUBLISHING if:
- The fee name, amount, formula, or applicability is not verified.
- Public page, checkout, quote, invoice, receipt, and support wording conflict.
- The wording sounds like tax/legal advice without owner/professional review.
- AI/chatbot answers would promise waivers, exact totals, or fee exceptions without human review.
- The policy owner has not approved the wording for publication.

Customer-safe wording examples

  • Checkout clarity: “Applicable taxes and approved fees are shown before checkout is completed. Some fees depend on location, payment method, or service type.”
  • Quote-safe language: “Your final total may include verified taxes, delivery, or service fees shown on the quote or checkout screen.”
  • AI-search guardrail: “Do not summarize fees as universal, waived, tax-required, or final unless the source page and checkout proof explicitly say so.”
