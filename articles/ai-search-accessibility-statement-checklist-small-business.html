Why this matters

Accessibility statements need verified facts, not optimistic AI copy

An accessibility statement can help buyers understand how to get assistance, report barriers, or request an alternate format. It can also create risk if it claims compliance, certification, remediation timelines, or legal coverage that the business has not verified. The goal is to publish a clear owner-reviewed source of truth that humans and AI answer engines can quote safely.

  1. Gather current facts: supported contact paths, response owner, known alternate formats, accessibility plugins or audits, caption/transcript coverage, and review dates.
  2. Separate intent from proof: say what the business does and how customers can report issues; do not invent WCAG/ADA compliance claims.
  3. Connect related pages: contact, support, privacy, booking, image-alt-text, video transcript, and trust-center pages should not conflict.
  4. Set a review owner: legal/compliance-sensitive statements need qualified review before publishing.

AI prompt for a safer accessibility statement draft

You are helping a small business draft or refresh an accessibility statement page. Use only the verified facts below. Do not invent WCAG, ADA, legal compliance, certification, audit results, remediation timelines, support hours, accommodation promises, or third-party vendor claims. Return: 1) a short plain-language statement, 2) facts that need owner/legal review, 3) related pages to cross-check, 4) customer-help contact wording, and 5) a stop rule before publishing.

Business/site: [BUSINESS NAME + URL]
Verified accessibility help path: [EMAIL/PHONE/FORM]
Known assistance options: [FACTS ONLY]
Covered pages/tools: [PAGES/TOOLS]
Open issues or exclusions: [FACTS ONLY]
Last review date and owner: [DATE + OWNER]
Related pages to cross-check: [CONTACT/PRIVACY/SUPPORT/BOOKING/VIDEOS/PDFS]
