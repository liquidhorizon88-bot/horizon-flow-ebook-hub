AI Search Source Citation Log for Small Businesses

If ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Copilot, or Google AI Overviews summarize your company, they need clear source facts to quote. This copy/paste log helps small teams track where every important website fact came from, who approved it, when it expires, and what AI must not invent.

Why this matters

AI search needs a trail from claim to source

Classic SEO often focused on keywords and page structure. AI search also needs clean, quotable facts: what you sell, where you serve, who you help, what proof is public, and which claims are not allowed. When those facts are scattered across old pages, PDFs, profiles, and marketplace listings, AI tools may mix stale information with current copy.

A source citation log gives the business a small source-of-truth worksheet before anyone asks AI to refresh a homepage, service page, product page, FAQ, comparison page, or local profile.

Copy/paste source citation log columns

  1. Fact or claim: the exact public statement, such as service areas, response windows, package names, or credentials.
  2. Source URL or document: the page, profile, policy, invoice terms, case-study approval, review, or owner-approved note that supports the claim.
  3. Source type: website page, Google Business Profile, marketplace listing, policy, review, license, case study, contract, internal SOP, or owner confirmation.
  4. Approved wording: the exact wording AI and humans may reuse.
  5. Do-not-invent wording: claims AI must not add, such as guarantees, awards, rankings, prices, certifications, or service areas.
  6. Owner: who can approve updates.
  7. Last checked: the most recent verification date.
  8. Review trigger: price change, policy change, staff change, location change, new review, new case study, or quarterly review.
  9. AI instruction: quote exactly, summarize with source, ask for verification, or do not use.

Five facts to log first

  • Service and product names: use the same names across homepage, service pages, product pages, and marketplace listings.
  • Location facts: cities, counties, remote availability, service-area boundaries, travel fees, and exceptions.
  • Proof facts: testimonials, case studies, awards, certifications, licenses, press mentions, and portfolio examples with permission status.
  • Offer facts: pricing factors, inclusions, exclusions, timelines, deposits, refunds, warranty terms, and availability windows.
  • Risk-sensitive facts: legal, medical, financial, safety, tax, employment, guarantee, or compliance wording that needs owner/professional review.

Copy/paste AI citation audit prompt

You are helping audit a small-business website for AI-search source consistency.

Goal: build a source citation log before rewriting pages or asking AI tools to summarize the business.

Inputs I will paste:
- Page text or URL notes
- Known service areas, offers, pricing rules, proof links, policies, and owner notes

Task:
1. Extract factual claims about services, products, locations, pricing, availability, proof, credentials, policies, and outcomes.
2. For each claim, identify the source URL/document if present.
3. Mark claims without a visible source as [NEEDS SOURCE].
4. Suggest safer approved wording only when the source supports it.
5. Do not invent statistics, reviews, awards, credentials, guarantees, legal claims, prices, or locations.
6. Return a table with: fact/claim, source URL/document, source type, approved wording, do-not-invent wording, owner, last checked, review trigger, AI instruction.

Human-review stop rules

  • Do not publish AI-generated citations or source links that a human has not opened and checked.
  • Do not use customer quotes, before/after claims, photos, or case-study details without permission and privacy review.
  • Do not let AI convert internal notes into public guarantees, rankings, legal claims, medical claims, financial projections, or safety promises.
  • Do not copy outdated prices, hours, service areas, staff credentials, or policy language from old pages without owner approval.
  • Do not use a competitor page as evidence for your own business claim.

Where to use the log next

After the log is reviewed, connect it to the pages that answer engines tend to summarize: homepage, service pages, product pages, pricing pages, case studies, claims inventory, and trust center.
