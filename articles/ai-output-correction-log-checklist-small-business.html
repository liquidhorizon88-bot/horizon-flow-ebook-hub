Why correction logs matter

Small-business AI failures are often small enough to ignore: a wrong appointment detail, an invented policy phrase, a missing source note, a tone mismatch, or a customer record summarized too confidently. Without a log, the team only remembers that “AI needed editing.” With a log, the owner can identify whether the issue is the prompt, the source data, the tool setting, the review rule, or the workflow itself.

STOP AUTOMATION: do not let AI declare the issue fixed, reduce human review, publish a correction, change a customer promise, or expand access. A named owner must verify the source facts, customer impact, correction wording, prevention step, and rollback trigger first.

Safe correction message snippets

Internal note: We corrected [workflow/output] because [verified issue]. Source checked: [source]. Owner: [name]. Prevention step: [prompt/source/review change]. Retest by: [date]. Do not expand this workflow until [evidence required].
Customer-facing correction draft for manager review: Hi [Name] — I want to correct one detail from our earlier message. The verified information is [correct fact/source]. Sorry for the confusion. We will use [next step] from here. [Owner/team] is reviewing the workflow so this does not repeat.
Pause decision: Pause [AI workflow] for [use case] until [owner] verifies [source data], updates [prompt/review rule], and reviews [sample size] new outputs without the same correction type.

Fast QA before reusing the workflow

  • Can a reviewer point to the source of truth for every corrected claim?
  • Did the fix reduce review effort, or just make the prompt longer?
  • Does the workflow now stop before customer-facing promises, privacy details, policy exceptions, pricing, refunds, warranties, legal wording, or safety-sensitive claims?
  • Was the same mistake found in any already-sent customer messages, saved templates, SOPs, chatbot answers, or knowledge-base entries?
  • Is there a fallback manual process if the workflow must pause?
