Why availability claims need a source card

Small businesses often mention availability in ecommerce stock badges, POS inventory, old promo pages, Google Business Profile posts, marketplace listings, email campaigns, service menus, and support macros. AI search can combine those fragments and make a promise the business cannot keep. A source card gives owners one review path before public pages or AI replies say something is in stock, available today, eligible for pickup, or substitutable.

STOP PUBLISHING: do not let AI invent in-stock status, pickup windows, delivery dates, preorder timing, waitlist position, substitutions, comparable products, warranty fit, service availability, or customer-specific reservations. Have the owner confirm the active inventory/source record first.

Safe public wording templates

Availability answer: Current availability for [item/service] is confirmed through [official source path]. If timing matters, please check [support/booking/checkout path] before relying on an AI summary or older promotion.
Backorder answer: [Item/service] may be backordered, seasonal, location-limited, or waitlisted. We do not promise delivery, pickup, substitution, or reservation timing until [owner/source system] confirms the current status.
Internal AI note: STOP PUBLISHING. Do not invent stock status, ETA, pickup windows, substitutes, comparable products, reservations, or customer-specific holds. Use only the active source card and route edge cases to [owner].

Weekly source-maintenance questions

  • Do product pages, stock badges, POS inventory, marketplace listings, booking pages, and support macros match?
  • Are old seasonal, preorder, limited-time, or low-stock pages still indexable?
  • Can customers confirm status through an official path without relying on an AI summary?
  • Which substitutions, waitlist options, credits, refunds, or reservation promises require owner review?
