Elevator Entrapment Emergency Response Checklist for Property Managers

A source-card worksheet for property managers, resident-services teams, maintenance coordinators, and portfolio operators handling elevator entrapment, stuck-elevator, elevator-alarm, emergency-call, fire-department, elevator-technician, accessibility, reimbursement, or resident-safety messages without letting AI or staff invent rescue timing, liability, medical advice, compensation, technical status, or legal conclusions.

ELEVATOR-ENTRAPMENT-EMERGENCY-CARD-READY

Use this before any AI-assisted resident update

STOP AUTOMATION: do not promise rescue timing, diagnose elevator faults, tell a trapped resident what to physically do beyond approved emergency instructions, confirm fire-department or technician arrival, assign blame, promise rent credits, reimbursements, legal outcomes, accessibility accommodations, or all-clear status until the property manager has verified the emergency-call log, elevator service record, vendor/fire-department status, resident contact path, accessibility/medical risk notes, and approved escalation owner.

  • Route active entrapment, injury, medical distress, fire, power, or trapped-resident reports to emergency/human review immediately.
  • Do not invent whether the alarm button was received, who is on site, whether the elevator is safe, or when service will be restored.
  • Do not promise compensation, refunds, rent credits, lease remedies, liability, or vendor fault in the first acknowledgement.

Copy/paste elevator entrapment source card

  1. Resident / location / elevator: resident name if known, unit, elevator bank/car number, floor or location, date/time first reported, channel, whether the message indicates active entrapment or post-incident follow-up, and any accessibility or medical risk mentioned.
  2. Emergency and source record: call-center log, emergency line note, fire department or 911 reference if provided, elevator alarm/vendor ticket, maintenance contact, front-desk/security report, camera/access log if authorized, and missing source facts.
  3. Safety and stop-rule notes: trapped occupant, injury, panic, heat/cold, power outage, fire alarm, child/elderly/medical-device risk, repeated outage, blocked accessibility route, or resident requesting compensation/legal answers.
  4. Routing boundary: property manager, on-call maintenance, elevator vendor, emergency services, security/front desk, risk/insurance owner, legal/accommodation reviewer, and who owns the next resident-safe update.
  5. Resident-safe wording owner: acknowledgement text, emergency contact instruction, facts that can be confirmed now, facts that are still unknown, next-update cadence, and official communication channel.
  6. CRM label: elevator_entrapment_emergency, elevator_vendor_ticket, resident_safety_escalation, accessibility_route_review, or stop_automation_elevator_claim.

Resident-safe snippets

Active entrapment acknowledgement

We received the elevator emergency report for [location/elevator if known]. Please use the property’s emergency contact process and follow instructions from emergency responders or the elevator service team. We are routing this to the on-call/emergency owner now and will provide only source-backed updates through the approved channel.

Post-incident follow-up without blame or compensation promises

Thank you for reporting what happened. We are documenting the incident, checking the emergency/vendor/service records, and routing it for manager review. We do not want to guess about cause, timing, compensation, or next steps until the source records are reviewed.

Accessibility or repeated outage concern

We understand this may affect access and safety. We are routing the report for manager review and will compare the resident need, building status, vendor record, and approved accommodation/accessibility process before confirming next steps.
