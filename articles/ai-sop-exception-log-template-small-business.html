AI SOP exception log template for small business teams

A prompt that works on Monday can still fail on a weird customer request, a missing input, a new policy, or a rushed employee handoff. Use this exception log to track where AI-assisted SOPs break, what humans changed, and whether the workflow should be kept, fixed, paused, or retired.

  • A team member skipped a required review step.
  • A workflow produced unclear ownership, stale facts, or risky wording.
  • You need weekly evidence before expanding an AI workflow.

Do not treat one good AI output as a finished SOP

Small businesses usually need a lightweight feedback loop more than another prompt. The goal is not to punish employees or automate every exception. The goal is to spot repeat failure patterns early enough to fix the workflow before customers, vendors, applicants, tenants, or owners see a bad handoff.

Human-review rule: any exception touching money, legal terms, hiring, medical/safety advice, refunds, deposits, contracts, privacy, customer complaints, public reviews, or final approval must be reviewed by the appropriate owner or manager before sending or changing the SOP.

Copy/paste exception log columns

  • Date / owner: who found the exception and who owns the fix.
  • Workflow: lead reply, customer email, review response, SOP draft, meeting notes, invoice reminder, onboarding, or other process.
  • Trigger: what input or situation caused the AI-assisted workflow to behave badly.
  • AI output issue: wrong fact, missing context, risky promise, unclear tone, wrong next step, privacy concern, unsupported claim, or stale policy.
  • Human correction: what the reviewer changed before using the output.
  • Root cause: bad prompt, missing input, outdated SOP, weak review rule, unclear owner, tool limitation, or user training gap.
  • Decision: keep, fix, pause, retire, escalate, or add to training.
  • Next check date: when to confirm the fix worked.

Copy/paste weekly review prompt

You are helping a small business review exceptions from AI-assisted SOPs. Use only the exception-log rows I provide. Do not invent incidents, customers, legal advice, policy changes, refunds, pricing, staffing decisions, or performance claims. Group the exceptions by root cause, identify repeat patterns, suggest one low-risk workflow fix, one employee training note, and one stop rule. Mark each workflow keep, fix, pause, or retire. Include a manager-review checklist before any SOP is changed.
