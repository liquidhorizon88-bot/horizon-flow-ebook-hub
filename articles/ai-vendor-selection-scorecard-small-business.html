Small business AI buying guardrails

AI Vendor Selection Scorecard for Small Businesses

Use this worksheet before buying an AI tool, hiring an AI consultant, or installing another automation. It helps owners compare real workflow fit, implementation effort, data risk, support, and human-review controls instead of buying hype.

Best for: small business owners, operators, office managers, and consultants comparing AI software, workflow products, prompt libraries, automation services, or AI-search/SEO services.

Important: this is not legal, security, procurement, or financial advice. Use it as an internal decision worksheet, then involve the appropriate owner, IT/security, legal, or accounting reviewer when the purchase touches sensitive data, contracts, compliance, payments, employees, customers, or regulated work.

1. One-page scorecard

Copy this into a doc or spreadsheet. Score each line from 0–2: 0 = weak / unknown, 1 = acceptable but needs review, 2 = strong / verified.

CategoryQuestion to answerScoreEvidence / notes
Workflow fitDoes this solve a specific weekly workflow, or is it just a general AI promise?
Buyer outcomeWhat measurable result should improve: faster response, fewer missed leads, cleaner SOPs, better AI-search visibility, or less admin drafting?
Implementation effortCan we test it with one small process in under 30 days without rebuilding the business?
Data riskWhat customer, employee, financial, health, legal, or account data would be entered?
Human reviewWhere does a person approve drafts, recommendations, customer messages, or website changes before they go live?
Fact controlDoes the vendor require source facts, citations, screenshots, CRM records, or website pages instead of inventing details?
Support / trainingIs there clear onboarding, documentation, support, or a template library a non-technical team can use?
Integration fitDoes it work with the current website, CRM, inbox, forms, calendar, or existing workflow?
Exit riskCan we export prompts, templates, SOPs, content, data, or reports if we stop using it?
Price clarityAre monthly cost, usage limits, add-ons, setup fees, and cancellation terms clear?

2. Stop rules before purchase

  • Do not buy if the vendor cannot explain the first workflow you will test.
  • Do not upload sensitive data until data handling, retention, permissions, and owner approval are clear.
  • Do not automate customer promises without a human-review step for prices, timelines, availability, refunds, legal terms, diagnoses, or safety instructions.
  • Do not replace your website facts with AI-generated claims unless a person verifies the exact service areas, hours, credentials, examples, guarantees, and policies.
  • Do not sign long contracts until a small pilot proves one measurable workflow improvement.

3. Copy/paste vendor comparison prompt

Use this prompt after collecting vendor pages, demos, terms, and your own workflow notes. Paste only non-sensitive information.

You are helping me compare AI vendors for a small business. Do not invent facts. If information is missing, write "unknown" and list the question I should ask the vendor.

Business type: [type of business]
Workflow we want to improve: [specific process]
Current tools: [CRM, inbox, website, forms, calendar, docs]
Sensitive data involved: [none / customer info / payments / employee info / other]
Vendor options and notes:
1. [Vendor A facts from public docs/demo]
2. [Vendor B facts from public docs/demo]
3. [Vendor C facts from public docs/demo]

Create a comparison table with these columns:
- workflow fit
- implementation effort
- data/privacy risk
- human-review controls
- support/training
- integrations
- price clarity
- exit/export risk
- questions to ask before buying
- safest 30-day pilot

Then recommend the lowest-risk pilot, not the flashiest tool.

4. 30-day pilot plan

  1. Week 1: choose one workflow and define the before metric: response time, drafting time, missed follow-up count, content update backlog, or support ticket backlog.
  2. Week 2: test on non-sensitive examples. Save the best prompt, SOP, checklist, or template.
  3. Week 3: let one person use it with human review. Track edits, mistakes, time saved, and customer-risk issues.
  4. Week 4: decide: keep, fix, pause, or replace. Do not scale until the owner can explain the review rule and outcome.
