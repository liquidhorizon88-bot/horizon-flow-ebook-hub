AI Tool Approval Checklist for Small Businesses

Before buying another AI app, use this one-page approval worksheet to decide whether the tool has a real workflow owner, safe data boundaries, measurable pilot success, and a human-review rule.

Best fit: owners, office managers, ops leads, agencies, consultants, and local-service teams comparing AI tools, chatbots, prompt products, or workflow automation platforms.

Why this matters

Small businesses often test AI tools because a demo looks impressive. The risk is not just wasted subscription spend. The bigger risk is letting a tool touch customer data, publish claims, change follow-up tone, or create invisible workflow debt before anyone defines who owns the result.

This checklist turns the approval conversation into a practical pilot decision: approve, approve with limits, test for 14 days, or reject for now.

The approval checklist

Approval areaQuestion to answer before buyingPass / fix / reject
Use caseWhat specific workflow will this tool improve: missed-lead follow-up, inbox triage, review response drafts, SOP drafting, sales-call summaries, or another named task?
OwnerWho checks the output, keeps prompts/templates current, and decides whether the tool stays after the pilot?
Data boundaryWhat data is allowed, blocked, or anonymized? Customer addresses, payment details, health/safety notes, employee records, and private contracts should not be pasted casually.
Human reviewWhere does a person approve messages, prices, policy wording, public claims, legal/financial details, or customer-facing changes before they go live?
Pilot metricWhat will improve in 14–30 days: response time, fewer missed replies, cleaner CRM notes, faster SOP drafts, support macro quality, or fewer owner interruptions?
Integration effortDoes the tool fit the current inbox, CRM, website, scheduler, spreadsheet, or team process without creating a second place to check?
Cost limitWhat is the monthly cost ceiling, and what result would justify keeping it?
Stop conditionWhat problem means the test stops immediately: hallucinated claims, private data exposure, unsupported promises, customer confusion, staff non-use, or no measurable improvement?

AI tool being evaluated: [tool name] Workflow to improve: [one workflow] Current pain: [what is slow, missed, inconsistent, or risky] Allowed inputs: [data the team may paste] Blocked inputs: [private/sensitive data never pasted] Output owner: [person/role] Human-review rule: [what must be checked before sending/publishing/saving] Pilot metric: [response time, drafts completed, errors reduced, etc.] Pilot length: [14 or 30 days] Cost limit: [$ / month] Stop conditions: [privacy, policy, legal, money, safety, customer-trust, or quality triggers] Decision date: [date] Decision: approve / approve with limits / extend pilot / reject for now

14-day pilot plan

Day 1: pick one workflow

Do not test the tool everywhere. Choose one repeating task with a clear owner and before/after measure.

Days 2–5: run assisted drafts only

Let AI draft or summarize, but keep human approval before anything reaches customers or public pages.

Days 6–10: track friction

Record where the tool saved time, created cleanup work, confused staff, or required extra review.

Days 11–14: decide honestly

Keep it only if the workflow got faster, safer, or more consistent without raising data or trust risk.

AI review prompt for the owner

Act as an operations reviewer for a small business. Evaluate this AI tool pilot card without hype. Identify: (1) unclear use case, (2) data/privacy risks, (3) missing human-review steps, (4) weak pilot metric, (5) integration friction, (6) cost justification, and (7) stop conditions. Do not invent facts about the vendor. If information is missing, mark it as a question for the owner. Pilot card: [paste completed card]
Human-review rule: this prompt helps find questions. It does not approve the vendor, legal terms, security posture, privacy handling, customer claims, or final purchase decision.
