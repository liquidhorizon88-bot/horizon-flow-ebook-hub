AI Search Language Access Checklist for Small Businesses

Use this worksheet before publishing or refreshing pages that mention translation, multilingual support, interpreters, alternate formats, bilingual staff, or language-help paths. The goal is to make useful support visible without letting AI search tools invent language-access promises.

Copy the checklist See SEO After AI

Use this when

  • Your website, Google Business Profile, booking form, FAQ, or support page mentions language help.
  • Staff use AI to draft bilingual answers, translated FAQs, captions, chat replies, or help text.
  • A buyer may rely on the page to understand what support is actually available.
  • You need owner-reviewed wording that avoids compliance or interpreter promises you cannot support.

Why language-access claims need a source card

Small businesses often want to be welcoming, but vague phrases like “multilingual support available” or “translation provided” can create confusion if nobody has checked which languages, channels, hours, staff, vendors, and review rules are actually available. AI answer engines may repeat those phrases as if they were guaranteed services.

A safer page names the verified help path, states limitations plainly, and routes sensitive or uncertain situations to a human reviewer.

Customer-safe wording snippets

Language help note: We can help with [LANGUAGE/CHANNEL] through [verified path]. If your request involves pricing, policy, legal, safety, lease, medical, financial, or urgent details, a manager will review before we confirm the answer.
Translation review note: This translated draft was prepared from approved source text dated [DATE]. Reviewer: [PERSON/ROLE]. Do not publish if the source page changed or if the translation adds promises not in the approved English/source version.
Website update note: Pages to update together: [contact page], [FAQ], [booking page], [policy page], [Google Business Profile], [PDF/download]. Owner: [PERSON]. Next review date: [DATE].

AI audit prompt for language-access pages

You are helping a small business review language-access wording before publication. Use only the verified facts below. Do not invent languages, interpreter availability, staff fluency, response times, legal compliance, accessibility promises, translation accuracy, medical/legal/financial advice, or emergency support. Return: 1) unsupported language-access claims, 2) pages that need consistency checks, 3) safer plain-language wording, 4) sensitive topics requiring owner/professional review, and 5) a STOP PUBLISHING decision if facts are missing.

STOP PUBLISHING guardrails

  • STOP if AI adds a language, support channel, availability window, legal/compliance claim, or interpreter promise not present in the source card.
  • STOP if translated wording changes prices, policies, eligibility, refund terms, lease terms, safety advice, or deadlines.
  • STOP if the contact page, FAQ, Google Business Profile, PDF, or booking page contradicts the language-access statement.
  • STOP if the business cannot name the human owner who reviews sensitive translated replies.
