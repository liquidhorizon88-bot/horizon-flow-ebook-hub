AI Search Homepage Checklist for Small Business Websites

Use this checklist to make a homepage easier for buyers, Google, ChatGPT-style search, Perplexity-style answer engines, and other AI systems to understand without stuffing keywords or inventing claims.

Copy the checklist Get SEO After AI

Use this when

  • The homepage says “solutions” but not exactly what you do.
  • Service areas, proof, or next steps are buried.
  • AI summaries miss the business’s real offer.
  • You want a safer first edit than chasing every new ranking rumor.

Why this matters

AI search needs unambiguous business facts

A homepage is often the page AI systems, search engines, directory profiles, customers, and referral partners use to understand a business. If the page hides the offer behind slogans, buries the location, lacks proof, or never answers buyer questions, the business becomes harder to quote accurately.

The goal is not to manipulate AI answers. The goal is to make the business clear enough that a human can decide what to do next and an AI system can summarize the page without guessing.

Copy/paste artifact

Homepage AI-search clarity checklist

Homepage section: [hero / services / proof / FAQ / contact]
Reviewer: [owner, manager, marketer]
Last reviewed: [date]

1. One-sentence offer
Can a visitor tell in one sentence who you help, what you do, and where or for whom it applies?
Example format: We help [customer type] with [service/outcome] in [location/audience] using [proof/process].

2. Service list with plain names
Does the homepage name the real services, products, or workflows customers search for?
Avoid vague labels like “solutions” unless the page immediately explains what they mean.

3. Service area or audience
Does the page state the city, region, remote audience, industry, or customer type served?
If there are boundaries, say them instead of implying every customer is a fit.

4. Proof points
Does the page include approved proof: reviews, case examples, years in business, licenses, certifications, process notes, before/after examples, or guarantees that are actually policy?

5. Buyer questions
Does the page answer the first five questions a buyer asks before contacting you?
Include fit, process, timing variables, price variables, preparation, and next step.

6. Internal links
Does the homepage link to deeper service pages, FAQ pages, resource pages, booking/contact pages, and trust pages?
Use descriptive anchor text instead of “learn more” everywhere.

7. Structured data and consistency
Do the business name, URL, phone/email, social profiles, service area, and organization details match across the site and major public profiles?

8. Human-review stop rules
Before publishing, verify prices, guarantees, legal/compliance language, service areas, licenses, health/safety claims, and customer proof. Remove anything the business cannot defend.

Implementation

Do the smallest useful homepage edit first

  1. Rewrite the first screen so the offer, audience, location, and next step are obvious.
  2. Add a short service list using customer language, not internal category names.
  3. Add two to four proof points that are already approved.
  4. Add a small FAQ block or link to a deeper FAQ page.
  5. Link to one free resource, one service page, and one conversion page.
  6. Run the human-review stop rules before publishing.

Human review rule: the business owner or responsible manager should verify any factual, pricing, location, policy, warranty, legal, safety, medical, financial, or performance claim before the page goes live.
